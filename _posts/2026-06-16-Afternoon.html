Summary: Open-source models are rapidly closing the gap with proprietary leaders in complex, real-world agentic tasks. This leaderboard evaluates models on millions of real-world, long-horizon agentic tasks, requiring systems to navigate web search, filesystem, and terminal tools to complete complex workflows (arena). Performance is measured based on task outcomes relative to the average model using a causal tracing methodology (arena). The progression from GLM-5.1 to GLM-5.2 highlights a strategic focus on execution reliability over raw flexibility. GLM-5.2 climbed from #13 to #10, driven by gains in confirmed task success and a higher ratio of user praise to complaints (arena). While bash capabilities and tool hallucination levels remained stable, the model exhibited a notable tradeoff in steerability, dropping 6.0% compared to GLM-5.1's positive 1.2% (arena). Despite these functional shifts, Zai_org has maintained the economic accessibility of the model, keeping the price identical to its predecessor at $1.4/$4.4 per million input/output tokens alongside a 1M context window (arena). For AI leaders, this development signals a maturation in open-source agentic capabilities, demonstrating that open models can now rival top proprietary systems in complex workflow execution. However, the specific tradeoff between task success and steerability in GLM-5.2 underscores a critical engineering reality: optimizing for autonomous task completion may currently require compromising on user-directed control. As agentic benchmarks increasingly become the standard for evaluating model differentiation, organizations must weigh the operational value of raw execution capability against the necessity of strict instruction-following in their specific deployment contexts.
